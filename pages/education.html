Spring 2020

Making Next to Forest | Toshiko Mori Studio Trip | Tokyo - Asahikawa - Higashikawa, Japan

studio considered a global approach to proposing alternative forest economies, with the goal of promoting increased local wood use ensuring a sustainable, ecological and economical future for Hokkaido, Japan. Ultimately, we designed a chair museum that would house the private chair collection of the Oda family—one of the largest and most prestigious collections of the 20th century in the Higashikawa township. The Chair Museum will be built with local wood and materials generated from the region, connecting the design to earlier research on forest ecology and bioeconomy centers.

Fall 2019

Groundless | Andrew Zago Trip | Charleston, South Carolina

The studio considered the progression of increasingly eccentric, atectonic works of architecture over the last fifty years—a progression that put into question the ground as architecture’s stable substrate—and contrast it to current reactionary formal impulses. We created alternative proposals for the International African American Museum (IAAM) that focused solely on the architecture itself as it negotiates between a hallowed ground/ meaning of the site and the interior program/content. The studio viewed this as a crystallized opportunity for architecture to negotiate the interrelationship of site, meaning, and content in the context of this important and evocative project.

Spring 2019

Recasting the Outcasts | Jeanne Gang with Claire Cahan Studio Trip | Woods Hole, Massachusetts

The studio proposed various design possibilities for existing concrete buildings, or what have come to be known as Brutalist architecture. The subject of the studio was the humble Swope Center on the MBL campus—a concrete structure in need of a repair, typological reinvention, and a new image. With a mindful approach to this structure’s past and present circumstances, we designed interventions that helped the MBL achieve its mission and position the new architecture to address typological relevance, sustainability, functionality, sea level rise and other environmental changes.

Fall 2018

Utopia/Dystopia: Living Post-Work | Annabelle Selldorf Trip | New York City, New York and Framingham, Massachusetts

This studio critically re-imagined the organizational forces of the built environment in a future where the structures that define our lives today – labor, education, leisure, dwelling – may look radically different. This allowed us to construct a humanist fantasy of communities organizing themselves in ways we can grasp but that have not yet been fully articulated within current social, cultural, and regulatory systems.
